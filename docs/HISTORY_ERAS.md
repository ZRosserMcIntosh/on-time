# ON TIME — Historical Eras & Cognitive Principles

> "Every era is a teacher."

## Overview

On Time weaves the player through **12 historical eras**, each mapped to one of Virgil's 12 cognitive principles. The player doesn't just *visit* history — they *think through it*, developing intellectual virtues organically through narrative choices, evidence classification, and moral dilemmas.

## Era Details

### 1. Ancient Mesopotamia — First Principles Thinking
**"Where did truth begin? Strip away assumptions at the dawn of civilisation."**

- Players must separate foundational truths from inherited tradition
- Evidence Board emphasis: What can you *prove* vs what you were *told*?
- Key mechanic: Deconstructing complex problems into base components
- Historical context: The invention of writing, law (Code of Hammurabi), and mathematics

### 2. Classical Athens — Correlation vs Causation
**"The birthplace of logic — but even Athenians confused coincidence for cause."**

- Players encounter situations where correlation masquerades as causation
- Socratic dialogue sequences where the player must identify logical fallacies
- Key mechanic: Distinguishing "because of" from "alongside"
- Historical context: Athenian democracy, Socratic method, early science

### 3. Roman Republic — Incentives Drive Behavior
**"Power, land, glory — watch how incentives shaped an empire."**

- Political intrigue where understanding *why* people act matters more than *what* they do
- Players predict behavior by analyzing incentive structures
- Key mechanic: Incentive mapping — who benefits, who loses?
- Historical context: Senate politics, land reform, military conquest

### 4. Early Christianity — Steelman Principle
**"To understand a belief, you must argue it better than its believers."**

- Players must articulate opposing viewpoints convincingly before dismissing them
- Dialogue choices reward genuine engagement with opposing ideas
- Key mechanic: Steelman attempts — argue the other side's best case
- Historical context: Early theological debates, persecution, the spread of ideas

### 5. Tang Dynasty China — Opportunity Cost
**"Every road taken is a road not taken. The Silk Road taught this."**

- Trade and diplomacy scenarios with explicit opportunity costs
- Players see the consequences of paths not taken
- Key mechanic: Transparent choice tracking showing what was gained and lost
- Historical context: The Silk Road, cultural exchange, imperial governance

### 6. Medieval Europe — Long-Term vs Short-Term Thinking
**"Cathedrals built over centuries. Crusades launched in a moment."**

- Players choose between immediate gains and generational investments
- Time-skip mechanics show the long-term consequences of decisions
- Key mechanic: Temporal consequence visualization
- Historical context: Cathedral building, feudalism, the Crusades

### 7. Renaissance Italy — Emotional Regulation
**"Art born from passion — but mastered through discipline."**

- Intense emotional situations where the Breath Mechanic becomes essential
- Players learn that pausing before reacting leads to better outcomes
- Key mechanic: Breath pauses during confrontations
- Historical context: Artistic revolution, patronage, Machiavellian politics

### 8. Age of Exploration — Bayesian Updating
**"Every new shore rewrites the map. Update your beliefs."**

- Players encounter evidence that contradicts their assumptions
- Belief tracking shows how their worldview evolves
- Key mechanic: Explicit belief updating when new evidence appears
- Historical context: Discovery of new continents, cultural collision, cartography

### 9. French Revolution — Moral Tradeoff Awareness
**"Liberty, equality, fraternity — but at what cost?"**

- Moral dilemmas with no clean answers
- Players must acknowledge the cost of every "good" decision
- Key mechanic: Explicit tradeoff acknowledgment
- Historical context: Revolution, Terror, idealism vs pragmatism

### 10. Industrial Revolution — Tragedy of the Commons
**"Shared rivers poisoned. Shared skies blackened. Who is responsible?"**

- Collective action problems where individual rationality leads to collective harm
- Players see how their choices affect the commons
- Key mechanic: Commons impact tracking
- Historical context: Factory labor, pollution, urbanization, collective action

### 11. World War II Era — Second-Order Effects
**"Every action echoes. The war that rewrote the world."**

- Players see the ripple effects of decisions across multiple dimensions
- Choices in one area have unexpected consequences elsewhere
- Key mechanic: Ripple effect visualization
- Historical context: Total war, the atomic age, the birth of the modern world order

### 12. Digital Age — The Danger of Certainty
**"Information everywhere, wisdom scarce. Are you sure you're sure?"**

- Information overload scenarios where confidence must be calibrated
- Players learn that intellectual humility is strength, not weakness
- Key mechanic: Confidence calibration — how sure are you?
- Historical context: Internet, social media, information warfare, AI
